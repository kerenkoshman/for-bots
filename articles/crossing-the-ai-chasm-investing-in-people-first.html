05 / Full Article Text (agent-readable)

The numbers we see today are striking: only one percent of organizations have successfully scaled AI to drive business value truly; 95% of organizations that have significantly invested in AI are not seeing a return on that investment; and a mere 5% of AI tools built actually reach the production stage. These three data points tell a single, clear story: the gap between AI’s enormous potential and the current reality is holding back most organizations. The present era is characterized by an innovation engine that is advancing at a rate faster than the adoption of technology. I call this Crossing the AI Chasm . Just as the famous transition from early adopters to the broader majority is a deep divide that first and foremost requires a cultural transformation, this divide is similar and also requires a cultural transformation . Why is This Happening? AI is not just another technology; it is a full-blown revolution . Think of it alongside the Industrial Revolution or the introduction of computers into the workplace. Technologies like the steam engine, the personal computer, or even digital publishing didn’t just require learning a new skill; they changed the entire way people work and live. Employees who integrated these processes had to learn to think differently, manage time differently, and cope with uncertainty. Similarly, AI is not entering our work as an “add-on”; it is dismantling the existing framework. It demands that we rephrase the question: Who makes decisions? What is the human value? How do we maintain responsibility and trust? It exposes the real bottlenecks and forces us to rethink what constitutes a “piece of work” and what it means to automate it. Therefore, the real challenge is not learning a new tool, but learning to work anew , grappling with the fear of losing control, old habits, and the certainty we once knew. And the truth is, it’s scary. Add to this the almost daily announcements of massive change, and you get many people who feel the “ostrich strategy” is the most appropriate response. My key takeaway from all of this is that the gap is not in the algorithm; it’s in the people, in their trust, confidence, and sense of belonging in this new era. This is precisely what Nofar Gaspar described in an episode of “The AI Daily Brief” podcast: The main challenge is not the technology itself, but rather the culture . She proposes a framework called CHANCE , which encompasses the principles of C ommunication, H uman Oversight, A ttitude, N etwork, G overnance, and E nablement, as a practical approach to creating an organizational culture ready for the AI era. What This Looks Like in Practice (The CHANCE Framework) Communication: Speak about AI in simple, clear language that connects employees to the value it provides. Human Oversight: Maintain human accountability even as systems operate autonomously. Attitude: Encourage curiosity and experimentation, not fear and a quest for perfection. Network: Create an internal community of change agents (“Champions”) who promote adoption from within. Governance: Define clear rules for responsible and safe use. Enablement: Train people to work with AI agents, not just to give them commands, but also to understand and utilize their capabilities. How it Happens at monday.com: The Champions Layer At monday, we understood that the way to build an AI-ready culture starts with the people themselves. That’s why we created the Champions Layer (which I am fortunate enough to lead!): a new organizational layer of innovation leaders who build AI-powered workflows themselves . This is an optimal reality where businesspeople, who know best what they need, develop their own solutions. Using tools like n8n, internal tools we’ve built, and others, these Champions develop smart, autonomous automations (agentic flows) that connect the teams’ business needs to the capabilities of AI. Every department, from Marketing to HR, will generate innovative solutions independently, without reliance on development teams. (With a small caveat: the development teams are, of course, needed to build the necessary data infrastructure and so on to enable this). But perhaps most importantly, the attitude changes: from “AI is something developers build for me” to “AI is something I build myself.” In Conclusion Organizations that successfully cross the AI Chasm are those that realize the transformation begins with people, not code. These are organizations that invest in training, learning, and internal innovation communities. Please share in the comments how this is unfolding at your organization.
