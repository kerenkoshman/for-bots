Reflections on careers in the AI��era I think January 26 was a turning point, both technologically and humanly. A growing sense of overload, and the realization that we’re at the very beginning of a revolution on a scale we haven’t seen before. Just today, Harvard Business Review published an article arguing that AI is actually making us all work much harder, even as we become far more efficient. A fascinating human paradox. Personally, I feel it very clearly myself. So let’s talk for a moment about what it feels like to be in a tech company right now. The idea, at least on paper, was simple: AI would take the routine work off our plates. It would write, summarize, debug, and free us up to focus on the things that really matter. Some CEOs even say they want their people to have more space, more time to rest. In reality, we’re filling the time freed up with even more work. There’s probably a name for this phenomenon. Expectations around immediacy and delivery have increased dramatically, far beyond the impact of AI on our productivity. In a study conducted at a technology company with approximately 200 employees, researchers found that people naturally expanded their roles as new capabilities emerged. As roles themselves are undergoing transformation and boundaries are blurring, product managers have started writing code, designers have begun opening pull requests, and people across a wide range of functions have started building their own solutions instead of waiting for internal engineering teams. It feels empowering, smart, and efficient, but it also accumulates into real overload. At the same time, the boundaries of work have blurred. It’s incredibly easy to start a task when there’s no friction and no blank page. A quick prompt during lunch, one more question before leaving the office, something running in the background. It doesn’t feel like work. Often it feels almost magical, like you can do so much more. But by the end of the day, you realize there was no real pause. Multitasking has increased as well. The feeling of having a “partner” working alongside you creates momentum, but in practice, it means more open contexts, more task switching, and greater cognitive load. We really are more efficient, but we’re also much, much more tired. The paradox the research points to is sharp. People are getting more done while also being busier. The cognitive cost of this new reality, combined with the constant attempt to keep up with a pace we’ve never experienced before, is very high. So yes, many of us are dizzy. I hear people say they wish they could just go to sleep for a year and wake up once things calm down. I understand that feeling deeply, and if you share it, you deserve a hug. Still, despite how I’ve opened this piece, I believe it’s critical to get on board with this revolution if you want to come out the other side relevant. This is where it connects to the job market and to careers. Not because of a dramatic wave of people being replaced by agents, but because of a quiet shift in how team member value is defined. Most of the layoffs we’ve seen recently are not the result of AI adoption, but of corrections, cost-cutting, and reorganization. Or, as it’s sometimes framed, putting “AI” in the headline to justify the cuts. AI still doesn’t do the work on its own, but it is changing expectations of those who do. This is noticeable across many roles. Product, marketing, research, recruiting, analytics, and more. People in these functions, with a strong technological orientation and capabilities, simply work differently. They rely less on others, move faster, and manage to execute ideas that previously got stuck. Not because they are necessarily better, but because AI helps them bridge technical gaps that used to be blocking. I see this very clearly in the layer of AI champions we’ve built at monday.com. Those who adopt an AI-first way of thinking, understand what different tools can and cannot do, and use them intentionally, generate significantly more value than those around them. This is already affecting demand. Organizations are beginning to realize that the team member of the future is one who has embraced the revolution and knows how to work with these new capabilities. At the same time, teams are being built smaller, not because there is less work, but because each individual can cover more ground. This is also influenced by the sharp drop in execution costs, which makes wasted time and excessive coordination more expensive. Work isn’t disappearing; it’s concentrating. More responsibility is held by fewer people, in smaller, more autonomous structures. This creates real tension. On one hand, there are more opportunities, more things that can be built, and more room for initiative. On the other hand, there is more load, fewer boundaries, and an implicit, and sometimes very explicit, expectation to hold multiple worlds at once. And still, there is good news here. We are truly at the beginning. The bar is still moving, the rules are not fixed, and most people are starting from a similar place. These capabilities can be learned, and the gaps can be closed. There has never been a bigger opportunity, and the information and knowledge are more open and accessible than ever. Those who choose to learn, experiment, and develop technological orientation alongside business thinking can significantly expand their options. Efficiency Went Up.
