05 / Full Article Text (agent-readable)

As product managers, you may have already noticed: working on AI models with data scientists is not the same as working on features with developers. The difference lies in the fact that models are not deterministic like regular code-based features. In other words, developing a model is almost always research work, requiring statistical understanding as well. I believe every product manager should know how models are built and how to “sprinkle” AI into their products (of course, only after deeply understanding your customers’ needs. AI is an amazing technological tool, but like any tool, it must serve the business. The path starts with a product manager who thoroughly understands the business. This post is for anyone taking their first steps in the world of AI-powered products. A Practical Example Let’s take a practical example to guide us: suppose you’ve decided to add an AI agent to your customer support team. You’ve mapped the customer and business needs and decided how you’ll measure the product’s business impact (say: an increase in NPS, reduced churn, or improving the ratio of agents needed in your call center to maintain a certain level of response quality). So, where do you start? Your proof of concept will be building a model together with your team that addresses those specific needs. And this is where evaluation comes in for the first time. One of the first steps is to define how we will measure the model’s quality . Why Is This So Important? Anyone can connect to the API of a major model provider (say, OpenAI), feed in a basic system prompt, and get a result. But that result will be poor. I highly recommend you try this yourself and see. That’s why, before diving into how the engineers and data scientists will build a model to solve the defined business problem, you must first determine how the model will be evaluated (in other words, the evaluation metrics are a core part of your definition of done). Example: Customer Support AI Agent In our case, let’s assume three criteria matter most when evaluating the AI agent’s responses: Product knowledge Understanding company policies Response style (How friendly, direct, or cold it is ��� in short, how well it matches the organization’s defined voice and tone) You’ll need to define the categories and what each score means. Human-Led Evaluation This should be something that a domain expert can score (say, the call center manager who already evaluates human agents’ responses and can rate quality). For this post, I’m setting aside the idea of AI evaluating AI — but if you’re building at scale and need a lot of examples, you’ll inevitably need a human-in-the-loop + machine approach. The Golden Dataset Here comes the second key concept: the golden dataset . This is a labeled dataset, created by a domain expert, that represents the team’s collective knowledge about what constitutes good responses. At this stage, you’ll likely encounter “debates” within the team — these are crucial for success. How many examples do you need? It depends on the use case. Statistically, the law of large numbers tells us that the more examples you have, the closer the average gets to the truth. A rule of thumb: around 500 examples. However, I recommend starting with 10 for internal sampling and then growing to at least 100. These are rough benchmarks and can vary significantly depending on the model being evaluated. It’s also critical to record not just the score, but the reasoning behind it. What was the human thought process behind giving that score? This data will help improve your models over time. Wrapping Up Once you’ve defined the criteria (and what makes for a “good score” in each), and built your agreed-upon golden dataset, you can start iterating on the model and improving it against those definitions. Pro tip: If you’d like to go deeper into your data science team’s work, I recommend exploring AI evaluation metrics such as Accuracy, Precision, Recall, and F1 score .
