A few months ago, I joined monday.com as the internal AI innovation lead. It was still a time when plenty of people believed AI was just hype, and more than a few looked at my career move the way you’d look at someone ordering a well-done steak at a Michelin restaurant. Questionable judgment, at best. I’d just wrapped up a tenure as Head of Product at a legal-tech startup, and my background is this somewhat unusual cocktail of engineering and product leadership. Fortunately for me, I’ve been obsessed with optimization and algorithms since I was a kid (yes, proud geek here, I was the one excited about graph theory, for example). And I was deep into what we now call “AI” long before it had the fancy rebrand. Since stepping into this role, two things keep happening. Friends and colleagues ask: “But what do you actually do ?” On the other side, organizations reach out to understand the profile, the responsibilities, and how to build something similar internally. So here it is , my attempt to answer both in one article, before my next meeting starts. First, Some Context: monday.com’s Big Bet To understand what I do, you need to understand the strategic shift behind it. monday.com has made a massive move, evolving from a work management platform into a Work OS. And no, that’s not just a slide in someone’s deck. It means monday.com is becoming the place where work gets done by agents, with humans collaborating alongside them. Think less “tool where you track tasks” and more “operating system where agents and humans actually get work done together.” That kind of shift doesn’t happen by accident. Someone needs to drive AI adoption from the inside out, proving its value internally before preaching it externally. Enter me as usual, overly enthusiastic. The Job: Four Pillars My role sits at the intersection of strategy, enablement, and rolling up your sleeves building. It breaks down into four areas: Business impact of AI. I work across every business department: sales, legal, finance, you name it, identifying where agentic flows can transform how people work, identifying the new role’s definition as the shift to an agentic workforce happens, and enabling the team to create the needed agentic workflows. The metric isn’t “how many agents did we ship” (vanity metric alert). It’s “how much did we actually move the needle?”. Governance and cost management. The less glamorous sibling. With AI tools multiplying across the org like tabs in a browser you haven’t restarted in weeks, someone has to own the big picture: spending, accountability, and making sure we’re not building shadow-AI chaos. Which models/tools are best for us is constantly on my mind, and I play with all of them! (and they pay me to do so!) Leading the community of AI builders (called AI Champions) . Honestly? This might be the most important one. I’ll get to it in a second. AI Market Intelligence. AI moves at the speed of “I blinked, and three new models dropped.” Part of my job is keeping a sharp eye on what’s emerging and translating it into an internal opportunity before it becomes yesterday’s news. The Part I Care About Most: We Can’t Be the Bottleneck Here’s the philosophical core of how we shaped this role, and the hill I will happily die on. My team is me, two brilliant solution engineers, and a growing army of agents. We work with every department. But if every single agentic flow has to run through us? We become the new bottleneck. And creating a new bottleneck in the name of eliminating bottlenecks is the kind of irony I’m not interested in. There’s a quote I keep coming back to: “Innovation doesn’t have a roadmap. You have to give people the psychological safety to try it.” AI is fundamentally a democratization play. The people who move the needle every day, the ones who understand the workflows, the edge cases, the weird Friday-afternoon exceptions, need to be empowered to build their own agentic flows. Not every solution needs an engineer. What it needs is someone who understands the problem deeply and has the tools and the safety net to experiment. So a huge part of my job isn’t building things myself. It’s creating the conditions: the tools, the frameworks, the training, and above all, the culture, where anyone in the org feels safe to try, fail, learn, and make their work 100x more efficient. That’s why the community of AI builders (called AI champions, business people from all departments without a tech background) is so central. We run education sessions, internal showcases, shared templates, and we celebrate experimentation. The goal is a culture where trying something new with AI isn’t a career risk, it’s Tuesday. So You Want to Build This Role? Here’s What I’ve Learned. Organizations keep asking me how to set up something similar. Here’s what I’d say, based on what’s worked (and a few things that didn’t): Hire a builder, not a slide deck strategist. This role needs someone who can prototype an agent on Monday morning and demo results by Thursday. If your AI innovation lead’s main output is decks, you have a problem. (No offense to decks, I just hate them personally and am SO glad there are agents that create them now). Find the product-engineer hybrid. You need someone who asks “why are we solving this” and can get into the weeds of how. A product leader with engineering fluency or an engineer with strong product instincts, that’s the sweet spot. Unicorn-ish, I know. But they’re out there. Don’t centralize everything. Resist the urge to make the AI team the gatekeepers of all things intelligent. Your job is to light fires across the org, not to be the only one holding the matches. Build enablement, not dependency. Set up governance before you need it. Less sexy than building cool agents, I know. But cost and governance get messy fast, and retroactive cleanup is the kind of fun nobody asked for. Measure outcomes, not activity. Nobody cares how many agents you deployed. They care about pipeline generated, hours saved, and decisions improved. If you can’t tie it to a business outcome, it’s a hobby project, and I say that with love, as someone who has many hobby projects. The Transition: From Product to AI Innovation People ask how a product leader who used to obsess over customers and value makes the leap into AI innovation. Honestly? The skillset transfers more than you’d expect. Product leadership is about understanding people, finding friction, and building solutions that create value. AI innovation leadership is the same thing — the solution space is just wider, the pace is absurdly faster, and the “product” you’re shipping is usually a workflow, not a feature. What’s genuinely different is the level of ambiguity. There’s no playbook. There’s no “best practices” blog post from 2019 you can follow. You’re writing the rules as you go. That requires a specific kind of confidence — not the “I have all the answers” kind, but the “I’ll figure it out, and I’m okay being wrong in public” kind. If you’re a product person considering this leap: your instincts are your superpower. The ability to ask “who is this for and why does it matter” is the most underrated skill in AI right now.
