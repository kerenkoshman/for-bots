As I sit in the lounge on my way to another cohort of Monday’s AI Champions, there is a recurring request that I should write about. So, what use cases do I see where Agents truly contribute? And what are the common problems when business people (without a development background) develop them? I’ll start by saying that I truly believe that the hour of democratization of building is a necessity. The fact that people responsible for the business’s bottom line decide and build agentic solutions for themselves, without the need for mediation (but with the need for a robust infrastructure), is a new world full of possibilities, where the potential for 10X growth lies for companies. Let’s start with: What is an Agent? An Agent is essentially a computer program that includes access to a Large Language Model (LLM), but unlike a simple chatbot, it can not only “talk” but also “do.” In simple terms, an Agent is an automated piece of code that knows how to plan actions, access external tools (like a CRM system, a database, or an operating system), and execute them autonomously to achieve a specific goal. And continue with a division into three levels: Agents that are Client Facing, where the SLA is critical. Internal Agents that touch Sensitive Information (e.g., Finance and HR systems). Internal Agents for Process Optimization. These three levels require tailored infrastructure, separate robustness, and, of course, different monitoring. Although what drives the decision of what to build in each category is, as mentioned, the business people who are responsible for the relevant business metrics. Use Case Examples: In Client Facing, this encompasses all aspects of customer support, as well as Top of the Funnel activities (for example, SDRs who make initial calls and perform lead verification). It is essential to note that these worlds require human in the loop at various levels, and an architecture that enables scaling and security, which are challenges that have not yet been fully addressed in the Agents world. In the HR and Finance worlds, these are Agents that optimize internal processes, but with the consumption of sensitive information. From an assistant for managers preparing for performance reviews with employees, to an Agent that answers questions about which invoices a particular supplier has and what their status is. I want to focus for a moment on these two examples. In the first example, there is sensitive employee information that needs to be verified to ensure it does not pass to LLMs and is certainly not exposed to unauthorized persons. Put proper controls and correct architecture in place before you let HR departments fly with their use cases 🙂 In the second example, if you’ve read this far, you know that it’s crucial not to put an Agent (which is ultimately based on statistics) into deterministic situations (meaning, where there is an absolute answer, such as the number of invoices a particular supplier has). So, where do Agents fit in these situations anyway? If some kind of decision-making is required in the process. Internal Agents without sensitive information — this is the category where I see the most blossoming among the Monday Champions. The current stage we are in is the optimization of existing processes (and I have no doubt that the day will soon come when we reach a complete redesign of processes, once the agent-building muscle is built). Here, we already have dozens of Agents in production with various use cases, such as an Agent that updates all required systems for every marketing event and another that collects information on all releases, allowing product teams to make informed decisions about beta, alpha, and other stages. The most relevant people to build the Agents in categories 2 and 3 are, of course, Operations people, many of whom built automations even before the era of Agents. And now we move on to the section on Common Problems: First of all, to enable all this beauty, you need a robust infrastructure. But… that doesn’t really exist yet in the world of Agents. So, what do we do anyway to let our business people run? We pay attention to the following things. First common problem: People do not understand what it means that the Agent is statistical. The fact that the results are not consistent and actually require their monitoring and verification. This is, of course, doubly important if it is Client-Facing agentic flow. How do we solve this? Provide observability and evaluation infrastructure, and of course, part of the enablement is teaching the subject. I also emphasize that if it is something deterministic, a simple automation without an LLM “brain” will be more effective. The second common problem is over-enthusiasm for this new technology, which leads to a desire to build gigantic use cases — the phenomenon of “Let’s solve the world’s problems with one Agent!” This approach typically leads to the context explosion of the Agent (the thinking behind it: simply provide us with the entire HubSpot database, for example, and we’ll obtain answers, and that’s it). And ultimately, disappointment with the result. To cool this down, while still allowing people to experiment and run, I recommend instilling the engineering mindset of breaking a big problem into small pieces and explaining the concept of context engineering — why you can’t give an LLM tons of information and expect it to perform magic with it simply. Third common problem: Maintenance of the Agents. Great, we’ve launched an Agent, and it even provides a solution that does a good job of streamlining the process. But what will happen to it in a week? What happens to it when it’s down? Don’t forget that you are in a situation where many people who did not come from the organizational culture of development are deploying Agents to production. Here too, the solution, in my opinion, is infrastructure that allows whoever manages the entire agentic workforce to track it, and on the other hand, provides coaching and training to people in the production world.
