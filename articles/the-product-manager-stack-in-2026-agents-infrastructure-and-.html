Hey PMs, this post is relevant as of March 2026 :) A lot has changed. Product managers are now expected to become builders and contribute to production code. But that’s not the only expectation the market is throwing at you right now. Since January, when LLMs made writing code a solved problem (and yes, that doesn’t mean you don’t need engineers — there’s a lot more to software development than writing code), the expectations from product people have gone X10. What matters most now is knowing what to build and deliver to market. That was always true, but now the cost of getting it wrong is multiplied — because everyone’s moving fast. Where does this hit you? You’re probably already under insane pressure because that’s just the nature of being a PM — one foot with the voice of the market (changing rapidly), one foot with the voice of customers (whose expectations are shifting just as fast), one foot in the world of analysts (how they see the market and the shifts in it), and one foot with the internal voices of the org (which is split, because you still need to maintain the “old” sales engine and customer base while transitioning to the new world). And all of this while companies like Lovable and Cursor are crushing it on ARR as we’ve never seen before. Demand for software and AI products is rising, so there’s also a massive opportunity. On top of all that, you need to figure out the right product strategy and ship fast while internal processes undergo an AI transformation. Not easy, to say the least. So let’s break down the stack that I believe every product manager needs in the AI era into two parts: 1 — Optimizing your day-to-day as a PM with a crew of agents and tools. What does that look like? And how does it turn you into a full-stack builder and your team into a meteor? 2 — Building AI products — when you build infrastructure instead of use cases, the world looks different. And remember: every agent is only as good as its evaluation… Let’s dive in. But I want to say at this point — these are my recommendations, and I’m more than open to criticism and conversation if you disagree or think differently 🙂 Optimizing Your Day-to-Day First things first — if you haven’t built up your literacy on how to use AI well, start there. Understand that this is a technology built on statistics, and what the implications of that are (hallucinations are a feature, not a bug…). But assuming you’re past that stage and you know how to get good results, there are a few agents you absolutely need to have (I recommend building them in Cursor, Claude (Code or Cowork), or as automations in n8n): One important thing: the real difficulty in using coding agents (Cursor and Claude Code) is the IT setup behind them. These are coding agents that sit on top of your repository (where your code lives), and there’s quite a bit of setup needed to get them working on the same repo your engineers are working on. It’s really, really important to do this in the same place, because the first thing you want to achieve is a unified context layer across the team. Why Unified Context Is the Foundation of Everything Think about it this way — if everyone on the team has their coding agent sitting on a different folder, or a different version of the code, or without access to internal documentation — you’re basically getting ten different versions of “what’s the right thing to do.” A PM who wants to build a quick POC or contribute code has to work in the same environment the team uses. So once that setup is working, here are the agents that help me most day-to-day: 1. Research & Discovery Agent The first thing every PM does (or should do) every morning is understand what’s happening out there. What competitors did, what customers are saying, and what changed. This used to be a manual process — reading blogs, going through G2 reviews, scanning Twitter and Reddit. Today? Build an agent that does it for you. What I recommend: an agent that runs once a day (I run mine in the morning), pulling signals from sources you care about — competitor reviews, social media mentions of relevant features, changes on competitor pricing pages, new posts in relevant communities. It filters noise, identifies what actually changed from yesterday, and serves you a short, focused summary. You can build this relatively easily with n8n, web scraping, and an LLM for summarization and prioritization. The key is calibrating the filters so you don’t drown in information but only get what’s actually actionable. 2. Analytics & Insights Agent I see PMs wasting hours sitting with analysts or building Looker dashboards just to answer one simple question: “How many users used feature X last week?” or “What’s the drop-off rate in flow Y?” Build an agent that’s connected to your data warehouse (or at least to the API of your analytics tool), and that you can ask questions in natural language. It writes the SQL, runs it, and gives you an answer. No waiting for the BI sprint, no opening a ticket. Question → answer → decision. This changes the speed at which you can make product decisions, and that’s the difference between a PM who reacts and a PM who leads. Of course — validation matters. The agent can return bad SQL, so always check the output against your intuition, and use it as a starting point, not as absolute truth. Pro tip: at Monday, this agent was built by our BI guild, who are responsible for the data. 3. Spec & Documentation Writing Agent Yeah, I know. We all hate writing PRDs. But they matter, especially now that you’re working with coding agents that need clear instructions. So what do you do? Build an agent that takes minimal input from you (short feature description, main user stories, edge cases you thought of) and generates a full first draft of a spec — including acceptance criteria, edge cases, open questions, and technical dependencies. The trick: feed it context about your architecture, your design system, and the conventions your team uses. The richer the context, the closer the draft will be to what the team actually needs. Instead of spending two hours writing a spec from scratch, you spend 20 minutes reviewing and refining. That’s a 6x factor on one of the most time-consuming tasks out there. 4. Rapid Prototyping Agent This is where the builder in you comes in. Got an idea? A hypothesis? Don’t wait for the next sprint. Use Cursor or Claude Code and build a working POC in hours. I’m not talking about production scalable code — I’m talking about something you can put in front of users and check if the direction is right. A PM who can build a working prototype and run it past 5 customers in a day, before a single line of production scalable code is written — that’s a real superpower. It doesn’t replace engineers. It lets you come to them with validation instead of assumptions. 5. Internal Communication Agent How much time are you spending on internal communications? Status updates, meeting summaries, internal release notes, stakeholder updates. I’m not saying the agent writes the final message — I’m saying it prepares a draft that you refine. It listens to the meeting (yes, there are tools for that), summarizes, breaks it into action items, and drafts an update for each relevant stakeholder in the right tone. That’s a l-o-t of time saved :) Building AI Products — When You Build Infrastructure, Not Use Cases Okay, this is part two, and this is the part where I see the most mistakes. So let’s talk about it. The Most Common Mistake: Building an “AI Feature” Instead of Infrastructure What I see over and over: a product team says “let’s add AI to feature X” and then builds one-point integration. Chatbot here, summarization there, auto-complete over there. And it works. For a moment. Then the next feature needs AI, and they build everything from scratch.
