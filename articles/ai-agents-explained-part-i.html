$30–40 billion has already been invested in GenAI (according to a MIT study), but most companies still struggle to achieve real ROI. Agents might just be the missing link between “cool demos” and products that actually work. So, what is an agent? At its core, an AI agent is a system that utilizes an AI model to interact with its environment, aiming to achieve a user-defined goal. It doesn’t just generate answers: it reasons, plans, and executes actions (often through external tools) to complete tasks. Think of an agent as two parts working together: The brain (AI model) — where reasoning and planning happen. It decides what to do next. The body (tools and capabilities) — the set of actions it can actually perform. The tools are like functions. The more functions you give an agent, the wider its range of possible actions. For example, if you’re building a travel-planning agent, one essential tool is the ability to check the weather at your destination. Without that tool, even the smartest “brain” can’t make weather-based decisions. Agents exist on a spectrum There’s a lot of confusion around what counts as an “agent” and what’s “just AI.” Large language models (LLMs) like GPT-5 (OpenAI), LLaMA (Meta), and Gemini (Google) are the most common brains powering agents. They excel at reasoning and generating text, but they can’t act beyond that on their own. When you ask ChatGPT to generate an image, it isn’t the model itself creating the picture. Instead, the developers added a tool — an image-generation function — that the model can call. That’s the magic of agents: combining reasoning with external capabilities. What can an agent do? In principle, anything you equip it for. Let’s say I want an agent to act as my personal assistant. I tell it: “Schedule a meeting with Dana tomorrow at 10:00.” Behind the scenes, the agent might use a tool like this: def create_calendar_event(participant, time): """Creates a calendar event""" ... When the agent decides to schedule the meeting, it executes: create_calendar_event("Dana", "2025-08-25 10:00") One action (“schedule a meeting”) may require multiple tools — checking availability, sending an invite, and confirming back to me. That’s the agent’s real power: chaining reasoning with execution. Real-world examples Personal assistants — such as Siri or Alexa — behave as agents when they parse requests, analyze context, fetch information, and act (e.g., reminders, messages, smart home control). Healthcare — Medical agents can track smartwatch data, flag early health risks, and notify patients or doctors, enabling proactive, personalized care. Gaming — Instead of static non-player characters, AI agents create dynamic characters that respond to players in context, making gameplay more immersive and unpredictable. Why this matters For product managers and builders, the shift is huge. What used to require massive systems and dedicated engineering teams is rapidly becoming standard functionality. Understanding how agents work isn’t just a “nice to have.” It’s becoming a core skill for anyone building in the AI era. Those who dive in now won’t just stay relevant — they’ll shape where the market goes. Next up: a deeper dive into agents, and what it was like to build one myself.
